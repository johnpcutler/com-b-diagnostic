# Social Opportunity Lenses

This file collects social-opportunity-oriented lenses for understanding `SO` in COM-B. It treats social opportunity as the relational, normative, institutional, and political conditions that make a behavior socially permitted, supported, and sustainable in practice.

These lenses overlap. That is fine. The goal is to make `SO` more diagnostically useful without losing the organizing simplicity of COM-B.

Within this repo's COM-B vocabulary:

- `SO` covers norms, politics, incentives, culture, and social coordination conditions.
- `SO` often interacts with `RM` and `AM` when social permission, legitimacy, or sanction risk shape motivation.

See [com-b-abbreviations-reference.md](../com-b-bcw-bct/com-b-abbreviations-reference.md) for the shared code system.

## SO-1-Norms: Norms and Normative Climate

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on what people perceive others do (descriptive norms) and what they believe others approve or disapprove (injunctive norms).

**Use when:** Revealing hidden social pressure that stabilizes or suppresses behavior independent of formal policy -- especially when local social reality contradicts formal intentions.

**Diagnostic question:** What does the local social reality reward or discourage?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on social-norms and normative-conduct traditions, especially Cialdini and related work in social psychology and behavioral public policy.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.1.1** Descriptive norm signal: behavior appears uncommon -> behavior appears common
- **SO.1.2** Injunctive norm signal: behavior appears disapproved -> behavior appears approved
- **SO.1.3** Norm clarity: mixed normative cues -> clear normative cues
- **SO.1.4** Norm consistency: leader message conflicts with peer behavior -> aligned messaging and behavior
- **SO.1.5** Enforcement intensity: weak social reinforcement -> strong social reinforcement
- **SO.1.6** Norm volatility: unstable expectations -> stable expectations
- **SO.1.7** Deviance cost: low social cost for deviation -> high social cost for deviation
- **SO.1.8** Practice entrenchment: practice is revisable -> practice is ritually entrenched
- **SO.1.9** Aspiration stability: organizational aspiration is durable -> aspiration follows trends

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: ED, EN, ER, INC, MO, PE, TR | BCT: 1.8, 1.9, 3.1, 6.1, 6.2, 6.3, 12.2, 13.2 | Dims: PC.1.6.1, RM.1.4.1, SO.1.1, SO.1.2, SO.1.4, SO.1.6, SO.1.7, SO.1.8, SO.6.1 | Cross: PC-1.6-Shared, RM-1.4-Identity, SO-6-Power

**Primary BCW linkage:** MO and ER, implemented mainly through BCT group 6 (Comparison of Behaviour) and BCT group 12 (Antecedents). MO addresses norm visibility via BCT.6.1 (demonstration of the behavior) and BCT.6.3 (information about others' approval). ER addresses the social environment via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment). EN supports norm-aligned action via BCT.3.1 (social support, unspecified).

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.1.1 (descriptive norm) is low but SO.1.2 (injunctive norm) is high, people approve but don't do it. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) is the priority: make the behavior visible. Avoid BCT.6.2 (social comparison) -- it can backfire by highlighting how few people actually do it.
- When SO.1.4 (norm consistency) is low -- leaders say one thing, peers do another -- BCT.6.3 (information about others' approval) won't work because there's a credibility gap. Use ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment) to align leader behavior with the stated message before deploying norm campaigns.
- When SO.1.8 (practice entrenchment) is high, the existing norm is ritualized. Frontal challenge triggers defensive reactions. Use PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to reinterpret the existing practice rather than trying to replace it directly.
- When SO.1.7 (deviance cost) is high, non-conformity is socially punished. Individual-level interventions (ED, TR, PE targeted at the person) are insufficient until the social cost is reduced via ER or EN.
- When SO.1.6 (norm volatility) is high, people won't commit because they expect the expectation to shift again. Stabilize the norm signal via BCT.1.8 (behavioral contract) or visible BCT.1.9 (commitment) from leaders before asking individuals to adopt.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, don't launch norm-change campaigns that require visible early adoption. The political cost will suppress uptake. Address SO-6-Power first.
- If RM.1.4.1 (identity congruence) makes the current norm identity-consistent, norm challenges feel like identity threats. Use PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to make the new norm identity-compatible before using MO -- see RM-1.4-Identity.
- If PC.1.6.1 (shared language) is low, people may interpret the norm differently. Align PC-1.6-Shared before deploying norm-based interventions.
- Norms often gate other interventions: if SO.1.2 (injunctive norm) strongly disapproves of the target behavior, ED, TR, and INC approaches aimed at individuals will face social headwinds. Resolve or neutralize the norm barrier before investing in downstream capability or motivation work.

## SO-2-Roles: Roles, Authority, and Boundary Clarity

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on whether responsibilities, decision rights, and role boundaries make it legitimate for someone to perform the behavior.

**Use when:** Revealing ambiguity and jurisdictional conflict that prevent action -- especially when behavior is technically possible but socially unauthorized.

**Diagnostic question:** Who is socially authorized to do what?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on role theory, organizational design, and jurisdiction/boundary work, including role ambiguity/conflict research and responsibility-allocation traditions in high-reliability settings.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.2.1** Ownership clarity: ambiguous ownership -> clear ownership
- **SO.2.2** Decision-right clarity: contested authority -> explicit decision rights
- **SO.2.3** Boundary permeability: rigid silos -> collaborative boundary crossing
- **SO.2.4** Escalation path clarity: unclear escalation -> reliable escalation path
- **SO.2.5** Role conflict load: frequent conflicting expectations -> low conflict
- **SO.2.6** Accountability fit: responsibility without authority -> responsibility aligned with authority
- **SO.2.7** Handback reliability: dropped transitions -> reliable transitions

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, ER | BCT: 1.1, 1.2, 1.4, 1.8, 3.2, 4.1, 7.1, 12.2 | Dims: PC.1.6.4, SO.2.1, SO.2.2, SO.2.5, SO.2.7, SO.6.1, SO.7.5 | Cross: PC-1.6-Shared, SO-6-Power, SO-7-Governance

**Primary BCW linkage:** ER and EN. ER clarifies structure via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment) to assign ownership and decision rights. EN supports coordination via BCT.1.1 (goal setting, behavior), BCT.1.8 (behavioral contract), and BCT.3.2 (social support, practical) for handoff protocols.

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.2.1 (ownership clarity) is ambiguous, nobody owns the behavior. ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring social environment) to assign explicit ownership. BCT.1.8 (behavioral contract) to formalize responsibility.
- When SO.2.2 (decision-right clarity) is contested, people avoid acting because they're not sure they're authorized. ER to clarify and communicate decision rights. BCT.4.1 (instruction) on role boundaries.
- When SO.2.5 (role conflict load) is high, conflicting expectations paralyze action. BCT.1.2 (problem solving) to negotiate priority among competing role demands. ER to reduce structural role conflicts where possible.
- When SO.2.7 (handback reliability) is poor, transitions drop the behavior. EN via BCT.1.4 (action planning) for explicit handoff protocols. BCT.7.1 (prompts/cues) at handoff points.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, people won't challenge unclear boundaries or claim authority. Address SO-6-Power first.
- If PC.1.6.4 (common ground) is weak, role negotiations fail because people don't share a common picture. Address PC-1.6-Shared.
- If SO.7.5 (policy-operational coherence) is poor, formal role definitions may conflict with operational reality. Address SO-7-Governance.

## SO-3-Network: Network Position and Social Capital

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on where actors sit in social networks, which affects access to information, trust, sponsorship, and coordination pathways.

**Use when:** Revealing unequal access and coordination bottlenecks that emerge from social structure rather than process design alone -- especially social-structure reasons behavior does not spread, sustain, or coordinate.

**Diagnostic question:** Who can access trust, information, and sponsorship pathways?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on social-capital and network traditions, including Bourdieu/Coleman/Putnam lineages and network-structure work on brokerage and closure (for example, Burt).

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.3.1** Trust density: low relational trust -> high relational trust
- **SO.3.2** Access to expertise: isolated actors -> well-connected actors
- **SO.3.3** Brokerage availability: few bridge actors -> multiple bridge pathways
- **SO.3.4** Network centralization: extreme bottleneck centrality -> distributed access
- **SO.3.5** Reciprocity norms: weak reciprocity -> strong reciprocity
- **SO.3.6** Sponsorship access: little sponsor protection -> strong sponsor support
- **SO.3.7** Cross-boundary ties: sparse cross-team ties -> rich cross-team ties

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, ER, INC, MO | BCT: 3.1, 3.2, 6.1, 6.3, 10.4, 12.2 | Dims: PO.2.1, SO.3.2, SO.3.4, SO.3.5, SO.3.7, SO.5.3, SO.6.3 | Cross: PO-2-Resource, SO-5-Legitimacy, SO-6-Power

**Primary BCW linkage:** ER, EN, and MO. ER restructures social connections via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment). EN builds access via BCT.3.1 (social support) and BCT.3.2 (social support, practical) such as mentoring or broker roles. MO spreads behavior across network boundaries via BCT.6.1 (demonstration).

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.3.2 (access to expertise) is low, actors are isolated from knowledge sources. ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring social environment) to create connection pathways. EN via BCT.3.2 (social support, practical) such as mentoring or knowledge-broker roles.
- When SO.3.4 (network centralization) is extreme, a single bottleneck controls access. ER to create alternative pathways. BCT.3.1 (social support) to redistribute access.
- When SO.3.7 (cross-boundary ties) are sparse, cross-team coordination fails. ER via BCT.12.2 to create boundary-spanning roles or cross-team practices. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) across group boundaries.
- When SO.3.5 (reciprocity norms) are weak, people don't return help. BCT.6.3 (information about others' approval) to signal that reciprocity is valued. INC via BCT.10.4 (social reward) for collaborative behavior.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.3 (political threat level) is high, network-building exposes people to political risk. Address SO-6-Power.
- If SO.5.3 (identity safety) is low across groups, cross-boundary ties feel identity-threatening. Address SO-5-Legitimacy.
- If PO.2.1 (time availability) is scarce, people can't invest in relationship-building. Address PO-2-Resource.

## SO-4-Incentives: Incentives, Accountability, and Reinforcement Architecture

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on whether formal and informal reward/accountability systems make the target behavior socially and institutionally viable.

**Use when:** Revealing misaligned reinforcement where the stated behavior is rewarded rhetorically but penalized operationally -- especially when incentive realities discourage behavior despite positive messaging.

**Diagnostic question:** What behavior do reward/accountability systems actually produce?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on organizational-incentive design, principal-agent traditions, reinforcement structures in organizations, and performance-management research.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.4.1** Reward alignment: reward supports target behavior -> reward favors competing behavior
- **SO.4.2** Metric validity: meaningful indicators -> gaming-prone indicators
- **SO.4.3** Accountability symmetry: balanced accountability -> asymmetric accountability burden
- **SO.4.4** Short-vs-long horizon: long-horizon reinforcement -> short-term pressure dominates
- **SO.4.5** Recognition visibility: contributions visible -> contributions invisible
- **SO.4.6** Informal reinforcement: peer recognition supports behavior -> peer reinforcement undermines behavior
- **SO.4.7** Penalty risk: low penalty for trying -> high penalty for trying

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, ER, INC | BCT: 2.2, 2.7, 3.1, 3.3, 10.1, 10.4, 10.5, 12.2 | Dims: RM.1.1.1, AM.2.2.5, SO.4.1, SO.4.2, SO.4.5, SO.4.7, SO.6.5 | Cross: AM-2.2-Reward, RM-1.1-SDT, SO-6-Power

**Primary BCW linkage:** INC, ER, and EN. INC restructures reinforcement via BCT.10.1 (material incentive), BCT.10.4 (social reward), and BCT.10.5 (social incentive). ER realigns the accountability structure via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment). EN makes performance visible via BCT.2.2 (feedback on behaviour) and BCT.2.7 (feedback on outcomes of behavior).

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.4.1 (reward alignment) favors competing behavior, the system rewards the wrong thing. ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring social environment) to realign formal reward structures. Don't layer new incentives on top of conflicting old ones.
- When SO.4.2 (metric validity) is gaming-prone, people optimize for the metric, not the behavior. BCT.2.7 (feedback on outcomes) using outcome-based rather than proxy-based measures. Redesign metrics to be harder to game.
- When SO.4.5 (recognition visibility) is low, contributions are invisible. INC via BCT.10.4 (social reward) and BCT.10.5 (social incentive) to make contributions visible and recognized.
- When SO.4.7 (penalty risk) is high, attempting the behavior is punished. ER to reduce penalty exposure. EN via BCT.3.1 (social support) and BCT.3.3 (social support, emotional) to buffer the risk.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.5 (permission to experiment) is low, incentive redesign itself is politically blocked. Address SO-6-Power.
- If RM.1.1.1 (regulation quality) is already controlled, adding more external incentives risks further undermining intrinsic motivation. See RM-1.1-SDT.
- If AM.2.2.5 (cue-triggered wanting) is high for the competing behavior, incentive realignment alone may not overcome automatic wanting. Pair with AM-2.2-Reward cue management.

## SO-5-Legitimacy: Legitimacy, Meaning, and Identity Safety

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on whether the behavior is seen as legitimate, meaningful, and identity-safe within the local social order.

**Use when:** Revealing whether people perceive the behavior as socially allowed, professionally credible, and reputationally safe -- especially the meaning/permission layer where people could act but do not feel socially authorized.

**Diagnostic question:** Is the behavior socially legitimate and identity-safe?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on institutional legitimacy traditions (for example, Suchman), social-identity and identity-work literatures, and social-practice-oriented work on meaning and normative order.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.5.1** Legitimacy signal: behavior seen as illegitimate -> behavior seen as legitimate
- **SO.5.2** Meaning coherence: behavior seen as pointless -> behavior seen as meaningful
- **SO.5.3** Identity safety: behavior threatens identity/status -> behavior is identity-safe
- **SO.5.4** Reputational risk: high reputational exposure -> low reputational exposure
- **SO.5.5** Professional congruence: behavior viewed as unprofessional -> behavior viewed as professionally appropriate
- **SO.5.6** Narrative support: no compelling shared narrative -> strong shared narrative
- **SO.5.7** Symbolic alignment: behavior culturally dissonant -> behavior culturally consonant

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, MO, PE | BCT: 3.1, 5.3, 6.1, 6.3, 9.1, 13.2 | Dims: RM.1.4.1, SO.1.2, SO.5.1, SO.5.3, SO.5.4, SO.5.6, SO.6.1 | Cross: RM-1.4-Identity, SO-1-Norms, SO-6-Power

**Primary BCW linkage:** PE, MO, and EN. PE builds legitimacy via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing), BCT.9.1 (credible source), and BCT.6.3 (information about others' approval). MO models legitimacy via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) from high-status or identity-congruent figures. EN buffers early-adopter risk via BCT.3.1 (social support).

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.5.1 (legitimacy signal) is low, the behavior is seen as illegitimate. PE via BCT.9.1 (credible source) to endorse it. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) from respected figures. BCT.6.3 (information about others' approval) to signal social permission.
- When SO.5.3 (identity safety) is low, performing the behavior threatens identity or status. PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to make the behavior identity-safe. This often requires changing the behavior's social meaning, not the person's beliefs.
- When SO.5.6 (narrative support) is absent, no compelling shared story explains why the behavior matters. PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to build a meaningful narrative. BCT.5.3 (information about social and environmental consequences) for the "why it matters" story.
- When SO.5.4 (reputational risk) is high, early adopters face reputational exposure. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) from high-status peers to signal safety. EN via BCT.3.1 (social support) to buffer reputational risk.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, no amount of framing will make the behavior feel safe. Address SO-6-Power.
- If SO.1.2 (injunctive norm) actively disapproves, legitimacy claims conflict with the operative norm. Address SO-1-Norms.
- If RM.1.4.1 (identity congruence) is low at the individual level, the person won't internalize the legitimacy signal. Work both SO-5-Legitimacy (social level) and RM-1.4-Identity (individual level) together.

## SO-6-Power: Power, Politics, and Psychological Safety

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on whether power relations and local politics permit voice, experimentation, dissent, and cross-boundary action without unacceptable social risk.

**Use when:** Revealing sanction risk, defensive routines, silence climates, and politically induced non-action -- especially active or passive suppression of behavior by political conditions.

**Diagnostic question:** Is it politically safe to attempt, discuss, and improve the behavior?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on psychological safety research (for example, Edmondson), organizational-politics traditions, and micropolitics/power analyses of workplace behavior.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.6.1** Voice safety: speaking up is risky -> speaking up is safe
- **SO.6.2** Dissent tolerance: dissent punished -> dissent tolerated and used
- **SO.6.3** Political threat level: high threat of retaliation -> low threat
- **SO.6.4** Conflict handling quality: conflict personalized/suppressed -> conflict constructively managed
- **SO.6.5** Permission to experiment: experimentation punished -> experimentation protected
- **SO.6.6** Status asymmetry: steep status gradients -> manageable status gradients
- **SO.6.7** Transparency of rules: implicit opaque rules -> explicit predictable rules
- **SO.6.8** Refinement openness: established practices are open to improvement -> established practices are closed to challenge

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: ED, EN, ER, MO | BCT: 1.1, 3.1, 3.3, 4.1, 6.1, 12.2 | Dims: AM.2.4.1, SO.1.8, SO.6.1, SO.6.5, SO.6.6, SO.6.7, SO.7.5 | Cross: AM-2.4-Helplessness, SO-1-Norms, SO-4-Incentives, SO-5-Legitimacy, SO-7-Governance

**Primary BCW linkage:** ER and EN. ER restructures power dynamics via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment) to create safe-to-speak formats and flatten participation structures. EN buffers social risk via BCT.3.1 (social support), BCT.3.3 (social support, emotional), and structured experimentation frames.

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, speaking up is risky. ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring social environment) to create structured safe-to-speak formats (e.g. anonymous channels, round-robin, pre-mortems). EN via BCT.3.3 (social support, emotional).
- When SO.6.5 (permission to experiment) is low, experimentation is punished. ER to create explicit safe-to-fail zones. BCT.1.1 (goal setting, behavior) framed as experiments with learning goals, not performance targets.
- When SO.6.6 (status asymmetry) is steep, junior people can't challenge senior practices. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) where senior people model accepting challenge. ER to flatten participation structures for specific decisions.
- When SO.6.7 (transparency of rules) is low, implicit opaque rules govern. ED via BCT.4.1 (instruction) to make rules explicit. ER to codify and publish decision criteria.
- SO-6-Power often acts as a gate for other SO interventions. If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is very low, don't start with SO-1-Norms (norm change), SO-4-Incentives (accountability redesign), or SO-5-Legitimacy (endorsement campaigns). People can't adopt behaviors that expose them to retaliation.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.7.5 (policy-operational coherence) is poor, power dynamics may be driven by structural contradictions, not individual leaders. Address SO-7-Governance.
- If AM.2.4.1 (perceived controllability) is low across the group, collective learned helplessness may have set in. Address AM-2.4-Helplessness alongside power reform.
- If SO.1.8 (practice entrenchment) is high, power structures are ritually reinforced. Structural ER is needed, not just behavioral interventions.

## SO-7-Governance: Governance Viability and Recursion (Viable System Lens)

**Core view of opportunity:** Opportunity depends on whether governance and coordination layers are organized so local units can act autonomously while still remaining coherently coordinated and adaptive at system level.

**Use when:** Revealing where either over-centralization or under-coordination makes behavior non-viable across scale, context, or change -- especially structural reasons a behavior works in pockets but fails to stabilize as a system practice.

**Diagnostic question:** Is autonomy/coordination/governance structured to keep the behavior viable at scale?

**Academic grounding:** Draws on the Viable System Model and cybernetic governance traditions, especially Beer, plus later organizational-design work on recursive coordination and adaptive control.

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **SO.7.1** Local autonomy fit: units lack local control -> units have right-sized autonomy
- **SO.7.2** Coordination sufficiency: weak cross-unit coordination -> effective coordination channels
- **SO.7.3** Escalation viability: escalation blocked or politicized -> escalation usable and timely
- **SO.7.4** Governance recursion quality: disconnected levels -> coherent recursive alignment
- **SO.7.5** Policy-operational coherence: policy conflicts with operations -> policy enables operations
- **SO.7.6** Adaptation bandwidth: weak sensing/adaptation -> strong future-facing adaptation
- **SO.7.7** Identity-boundary clarity: unclear system purpose/boundary -> clear identity and boundary
- **SO.7.8** Cross-group consistency: consistent practice across groups -> high practice variation across groups
- **SO.7.9** Convergence direction: groups are converging on shared practice -> groups are diverging further

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, ER, MO | BCT: 1.1, 2.2, 2.7, 3.2, 4.4, 6.1, 12.2 | Dims: PC.1.6.4, PO.7.7, SO.6.1, SO.7.1, SO.7.2, SO.7.5, SO.7.8, SO.7.9 | Cross: PC-1.6-Shared, PO-7-Control, SO-6-Power

**Primary BCW linkage:** ER and EN. ER restructures governance via BCT.12.2 (restructuring the social environment) to delegate autonomy and improve coordination channels. EN supports adaptive governance via BCT.2.2 (feedback on behaviour) and BCT.2.7 (feedback on outcomes of behavior) shared across levels.

**Shaping notes:**
- When SO.7.1 (local autonomy fit) is low, units lack right-sized autonomy. ER via BCT.12.2 (restructuring social environment) to delegate decision rights. BCT.1.1 (goal setting, behavior) at the local level with local ownership.
- When SO.7.2 (coordination sufficiency) is weak, cross-unit coordination fails. EN via BCT.3.2 (social support, practical) through coordination roles or liaison functions. BCT.2.2 (feedback on behaviour) shared across units.
- When SO.7.5 (policy-operational coherence) is poor, policy conflicts with operations. ER to revise policy through operational consultation. BCT.4.4 (behavioral experiments) to test policy changes before system-wide rollout.
- When SO.7.8 (cross-group consistency) is low and SO.7.9 (convergence direction) is diverging, groups are drifting apart. MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) of shared practices across groups. EN via BCT.1.1 (goal setting) for convergence targets. But check whether variation is adaptive before forcing consistency.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, governance reform proposals will be suppressed. Address SO-6-Power.
- If PC.1.6.4 (common ground) is weak, governance discussions devolve because groups don't share concepts. Address PC-1.6-Shared.
- If PO.7.7 (variety handling) is exceeded -- variability exceeds control capacity -- governance structures need more variety, not less. The fix is not tighter control but better requisite-variety matching. See PO-7-Control.

## From Social Opportunity Lenses To COM-B / BCW

These lenses do not replace COM-B. They help specify what kind of `SO` problem is inside the broad `Opportunity` branch.

- Most of these lenses primarily refine `SO`.
- Secondary effects often appear in `RM` (identity, value, intention shifts) and `AM` (fear-based avoidance, defensive habits).

Once dominant social-opportunity patterns are clearer, the next step is usually to move into [com-b-to-bcw-intervention-function-mapping.md](../com-b-bcw-bct/com-b-to-bcw-intervention-function-mapping.md):

- `SO` is most often paired with `RE`, `ER`, `MO`, and `EN`.

The goal is not to duplicate that mapping here, but to make it easier to move from social-opportunity diagnosis to intervention family.

## Boundaries of this file

This note is intentionally social-opportunity-first. It does not fully address:

- time, tools, workflow, and physical setting constraints
- cognitive skill and physical skill capability limits
- internal motivational dynamics such as habits, affect, or self-efficacy beliefs

Those belong more naturally in adjacent notes such as [physical-opportunity-lenses.md](physical-opportunity-lenses.md), [capability-lenses.md](capability-lenses.md), and [motivation-lenses.md](motivation-lenses.md).

Important distinctions:

- `SO` is not `RM`
  Social permission and institutional legitimacy are external conditions, while reflective endorsement is internal motivation.
- `SO` is not `AM`
  Fear and avoidance habits can be downstream effects, but the social sanction structure is the opportunity condition.
- `SO` is not `PO`
  Political and normative barriers differ from tool/time/workflow barriers.

Read next:
- [physical-opportunity-lenses.md](physical-opportunity-lenses.md) for `PO` conditions
- [motivation-lenses.md](motivation-lenses.md) for `RM` and `AM` effects downstream of social conditions

## References To Ground These Lenses

- **COM-B and BCW:** Michie, van Stralen, and West on COM-B and the Behaviour Change Wheel.
- **Norms and Normative Climate:** Cialdini, Reno, and Kallgren on normative conduct; social-norm intervention literature.
- **Roles, Authority, and Boundary Clarity:** role ambiguity/conflict traditions (for example, Kahn et al.); jurisdiction and boundary-work traditions.
- **Network Position and Social Capital:** Bourdieu, Coleman, and Putnam on social capital; Burt on structural holes and brokerage.
- **Incentives, Accountability, and Reinforcement Architecture:** principal-agent and organizational incentive-alignment traditions; research on metric design and unintended consequences.
- **Legitimacy, Meaning, and Identity Safety:** Suchman on legitimacy; social identity and identity-work traditions; practice theory work on meaning and normative order.
- **Power, Politics, and Psychological Safety:** Edmondson on psychological safety; organizational politics and power relations in workplace behavior.
- **Governance Viability and Recursion (Viable System Lens):** Beer on the Viable System Model; cybernetic governance and recursive organizational design traditions.
