# Motivation Lenses

This file collects motivation-oriented lenses for understanding the `M` in COM-B. It treats `Reflective Motivation` and `Automatic Motivation` as the two top-level buckets, then uses established sub-lenses to unpack what each one may contain. These lenses overlap. That is fine. The goal is to make motivation more descriptively useful without losing the organizing simplicity of COM-B.

The note is grounded in COM-B and its link to PRIME Theory:

- `Reflective Motivation` (`RM`) covers plans, evaluations, beliefs, identity-linked reasons, and deliberate choice.
- `Automatic Motivation` (`AM`) covers habits, impulses, affective reactions, learned responses, and inhibition.

This note therefore refines the motivation branch of the shared COM-B vocabulary rather than replacing it. See [com-b-abbreviations-reference.md](../com-b-bcw-bct/com-b-abbreviations-reference.md) for the broader code system.

## 1. Reflective Motivation

Reflective motivation concerns the more deliberate, evaluative, and self-conscious side of action. It is strongest when the diagnostic question is some version of:

- Do people believe this is worth doing?
- Do they believe they can do it?
- Does it fit who they are?
- Have they turned the intention into a usable plan?

### RM-1.1-SDT: Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

**Core view of motivation:** Motivation varies in quality, not just quantity. Behavior is more robust when it is autonomously endorsed and when the context supports autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

**Use when:** Revealing whether people are acting from choice and internalization versus pressure, compliance, or controlled obligation -- especially the difference between fragile compliance and durable commitment when motivation sounds present but is externally driven.

**Diagnostic question:** Is the motivation self-endorsed or mainly controlled?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **RM.1.1.1** Regulation quality: controlled -> autonomous
- **RM.1.1.2** Need satisfaction: frustrated needs -> supported needs
- **RM.1.1.3** Autonomy support: coercive context -> autonomy-supportive context
- **RM.1.1.4** Competence support: discouraging or overwhelming -> confidence-building and optimally challenging
- **RM.1.1.5** Relatedness support: socially disconnected -> socially supported
- **RM.1.1.6** Internalization: surface compliance -> integrated endorsement
- **RM.1.1.7** Goal self-concordance: alien goals -> self-concordant goals

- If AM.2.4.1 (perceived controllability) is low, the person has given up and autonomy support feels irrelevant. Address AM-2.4-Helplessness before SDT-oriented work.
- If PC.1.1.1 (declarative knowledge) is very low, the person can't form a meaningful autonomous relationship with a behavior they don't understand. Address PC-1.1-Knowledge first.

### RM-1.2-Efficacy: Self-Efficacy

**Core view of motivation:** Belief in one's capability to execute the behavior strongly influences initiation, effort, persistence, and recovery after setbacks.

**Use when:** Revealing the motivational effect of perceived capability rather than actual capability alone -- especially when people value the behavior but doubt their ability to carry it through.

**Diagnostic question:** Do people believe they can execute the behavior?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **RM.1.2.1** Efficacy belief: low confidence -> high confidence
- **RM.1.2.2** Persistence under difficulty: quick withdrawal -> sustained effort
- **RM.1.2.3** Recovery after setbacks: fragile -> resilient
- **RM.1.2.4** Task specificity: broad uncertainty -> specific confidence for the target behavior
- **RM.1.2.5** Transfer confidence: confidence is narrow -> confidence generalizes across settings
- **RM.1.2.6** Source of efficacy: weak mastery/vicarious/persuasion support -> strong efficacy-building experience
- **RM.1.2.7** Emotional interpretation: arousal feels disabling -> arousal feels manageable or mobilizing
- **RM.1.2.8** Change efficacy: confident the behavior can be safely changed -> changing the behavior feels dangerous

- When RM.1.2.4 (task specificity) is broad uncertainty, confidence is diffusely low. BCT.1.1 (goal setting, behavior) to narrow the target; BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) to build specific mastery experiences.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If AM.2.4.1 (perceived controllability) is very low and AM.2.4.7 (exposure duration) is prolonged, the issue has progressed beyond low efficacy into learned helplessness. PE alone won't reach it. Address AM-2.4-Helplessness with BCT.4.3 (re-attribution) and restored controllability.
- If SO.6.5 (permission to experiment) is low, the person can't build mastery experiences in a punitive environment. Address SO-6-Power.
- If PC.1.1.4 (skill stage) is genuinely novice, efficacy is appropriately low -- the person correctly perceives they can't do it yet. Don't try to persuade capability that doesn't exist. Address PC-1.1-Knowledge with TR first; efficacy will follow mastery.

### RM-1.3-EVC: Expectancy-Value-Cost

**Core view of motivation:** Motivation depends on whether success seems likely enough, valuable enough, and low-cost enough to justify action.

**Use when:** Revealing the reflective calculus behind "not worth it," "too costly," or "unlikely to pay off" -- especially tradeoffs and cost-heavy behaviors that fail not from incapacity but because they do not seem worth the effort.

**Diagnostic question:** Does the behavior seem worth doing?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **RM.1.3.1** Expectancy of success: unlikely -> likely
- **RM.1.3.2** Attainment value: unimportant -> important
- **RM.1.3.3** Interest value: uninteresting -> intrinsically engaging
- **RM.1.3.4** Utility value: low practical benefit -> high practical benefit
- **RM.1.3.5** Effort cost: low effort burden -> high effort burden
- **RM.1.3.6** Emotional cost: low emotional burden -> high emotional burden
- **RM.1.3.7** Opportunity cost: little sacrificed -> substantial sacrifice
- **RM.1.3.8** Present-vs-future weighting: future value dominates -> present cost dominates

ED makes consequences visible via BCT.5.1 (information about health consequences) and BCT.5.3 (information about social and environmental consequences). INC bridges temporal gaps via BCT.10.1 (material incentive) and BCT.10.4 (social reward).

**Shaping notes:**
- When RM.1.3.1 (expectancy of success) is low, the person doesn't think it will work. PE via BCT.9.1 (credible source) and BCT.5.1 (information about consequences) with evidence of effectiveness. BCT.6.1 (demonstration) from successful peers.
- When RM.1.3.5 (effort cost) or RM.1.3.6 (emotional cost) is high, the behavior is "too expensive." Don't just increase value -- reduce cost. EN via PO-level friction reduction (see PO-3-Workflow). BCT.11.2 (reduce negative emotions) for emotional cost.
- When RM.1.3.8 (present-vs-future weighting) favors present cost over future benefit, delayed rewards are discounted. BCT.5.2 (salience of consequences) to make future outcomes vivid. BCT.9.3 (comparative imagining of future outcomes). INC via BCT.10.1 (material incentive) to bridge the temporal gap with proximal reward.
- When RM.1.3.7 (opportunity cost) is high, the person has better things to do. No amount of PE changes this if the competing activity is genuinely more valuable. Re-examine whether the target behavior is the right one, or redesign it to reduce opportunity cost.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If PO.2.1 (time availability) is very low, effort cost is driven by real scarcity, not perception. Address PO-2-Resource before PE.
- If SO.4.1 (reward alignment) punishes the behavior, the cost calculus is rational. Don't try to persuade people past rational cost-benefit. Fix SO-4-Incentives.
- If RM.1.2.1 (efficacy belief) is low, expectancy of success is driven by efficacy doubt. Address RM-1.2-Efficacy before trying to reframe value.

### RM-1.4-Identity: Identity-Based Motivation

**Core view of motivation:** People are more ready to act when the behavior feels congruent with a salient identity and when difficulty is interpreted as meaningful rather than disqualifying.

**Use when:** Revealing whether the behavior feels like something "someone like me" does -- especially aspirational but weakly realized behaviors, professionalism questions, and resistance rooted in self-definition rather than simple preference.

**Diagnostic question:** Does the behavior fit who the person is or wants to be?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **RM.1.4.1** Identity congruence: behavior feels alien -> behavior feels identity-consistent
- **RM.1.4.2** Prototypicality: behavior feels marginal to the identity -> behavior feels central to it
- **RM.1.4.3** Identity conflict: few competing identities -> many competing identities
- **RM.1.4.4** Interpretation of difficulty: difficulty signals mismatch -> difficulty signals importance
- **RM.1.4.5** Future-self pull: weak possible self -> strong possible self
- **RM.1.4.6** Authenticity: performative adoption -> authentic endorsement
- **RM.1.4.7** Reputation risk: low identity exposure -> high identity exposure
- **RM.1.4.8** Identity-practice distance: identity and practice are aligned -> aspiration far exceeds practice

**Primary BCW linkage:** PE and MO. PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to connect behavior to valued identity, BCT.13.5 (identity associated with changed behavior), and BCT.13.4 (valued self-identity). MO via BCT.6.1 (demonstration) from identity-congruent models.

**Shaping notes:**
- When RM.1.4.1 (identity congruence) is low, the behavior feels alien. PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to connect the behavior to an identity the person values. BCT.13.5 (identity associated with changed behavior) to help construct a new self-concept.
- When RM.1.4.4 (interpretation of difficulty) reads difficulty as mismatch, difficulty signals "this isn't for me." PE via BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) difficulty as evidence of growth. BCT.15.3 (focus on past success) to counter the mismatch interpretation.
- When RM.1.4.3 (identity conflict) involves many competing identities, the behavior serves one identity but threatens another. BCT.13.4 (valued self-identity) to affirm the broader self; BCT.9.2 (pros and cons) for explicit identity negotiation.
- When RM.1.4.8 (identity-practice distance) is large, aspiration far exceeds practice. BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) to close the gap incrementally; BCT.1.4 (action planning) for concrete steps toward the aspired identity.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.5.3 (identity safety) is low, the behavior threatens the person's social standing. Individual identity work won't overcome social identity threat. Address SO-5-Legitimacy first.
- If SO.1.2 (injunctive norm) signals disapproval of the identity the behavior represents, PE via identity-building will be undermined by social punishment. Address SO-1-Norms.
- If AM.2.3.5 (social-emotional intensity) is high with shame, identity work risks triggering shame spirals. Address AM-2.3-Affect with BCT.11.2 first.

### RM-1.5-Intentions: Goal Intentions and Implementation Intentions

**Core view of motivation:** Wanting a behavior is not the same as having converted it into a sufficiently specific plan for action.

**Use when:** Revealing the gap between intention and action -- especially when people genuinely intend to act but fail at initiation, timing, or follow-through.

**Diagnostic question:** Has intention been translated into actionable plans?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **RM.1.5.1** Intention strength: weak goal commitment -> strong goal commitment
- **RM.1.5.2** Intention stability: fluctuating intention -> stable intention
- **RM.1.5.3** Plan specificity: vague intention -> precise if-then planning
- **RM.1.5.4** Cue linkage: no clear trigger -> strong cue-response linkage
- **RM.1.5.5** Obstacle planning: no coping plan -> explicit coping plan
- **RM.1.5.6** Initiation readiness: hard to start -> easy to start
- **RM.1.5.7** Maintenance planning: weak follow-through structure -> strong follow-through structure

- When RM.1.5.1 (intention strength) is weak, goal commitment is low. PE via BCT.9.2 (pros and cons) and BCT.5.5 (anticipated regret) to strengthen commitment. BCT.1.9 (commitment) to formalize.
- When RM.1.5.3 (plan specificity) is vague, the person intends but hasn't specified when, where, how. EN via BCT.1.4 (action planning) for if-then plans. This is the critical bridge to BCT.8.3 (habit formation) in AM-2.1-Habit.

- When RM.1.5.6 (initiation readiness) is low, the person can't start. BCT.1.4 (action planning) with specific start triggers. EN via BCT.7.1 (prompts/cues) to externalize initiation.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If RM.1.3.5 (effort cost) is genuinely high, planning can't overcome a legitimate cost barrier. Address the cost via PO-level friction reduction or RM-1.3-EVC reframing first.
- If PO.3.7 (activation energy) is high, the intention-action gap may be environmental, not motivational. Address PO-3-Workflow.
- If AM.2.1.7 (competing habit strength) is high, well-formed plans may lose to automatic routines. Pair intention work with AM-2.1-Habit disruption strategies.

## 2. Automatic Motivation

Automatic motivation concerns the faster, more associative, more emotional, and less deliberative side of behavior. It is strongest when the diagnostic question is some version of:

- What does the person do on autopilot?
- What gets triggered by the context?
- What feels attractive, aversive, or relieving in the moment?
- Why has the person stopped trying?

### AM-2.1-Habit: Habit Formation

**Core view of motivation:** Repeated behavior in stable contexts creates context-response associations that can drive action with minimal deliberation.

**Use when:** Revealing autopilot behavior and why changing intentions often fails to change repeated action -- especially routine, inertia, and persistence of established behavior despite attitude or plan change.

**Diagnostic question:** What does the person do on autopilot?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **AM.2.1.1** Automaticity: effortful and deliberate -> automatic and effortless
- **AM.2.1.2** Context dependence: weak cueing -> strong cue-triggering
- **AM.2.1.3** Repetition history: little repetition -> extensive repetition
- **AM.2.1.4** Context stability: unstable context -> stable context
- **AM.2.1.5** Goal independence: behavior follows current goals -> behavior runs despite current goals
- **AM.2.1.6** Disruption sensitivity: easy to disrupt -> highly entrenched
- **AM.2.1.7** Competing habit strength: weak competing routine -> strong competing routine

**Shaping notes:**
- When AM.2.1.1 (automaticity) is high and AM.2.1.5 (goal independence) is high, the behavior runs despite current goals. Intent-based interventions (PE, ED) won't reach it. Use ER via BCT.12.1 (restructuring the physical environment) and BCT.12.3 (avoidance/reducing exposure to cues) to disrupt the cue-response chain.
- When AM.2.1.4 (context stability) is high, two strategies diverge. - When AM.2.1.6 (disruption sensitivity) is low and AM.2.1.3 (repetition history) is extensive, expect slow change. Use BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) and BCT.14.4 (reward approximation) to shape incrementally rather than expecting sudden replacement.
- When AM.2.1.2 (context dependence) is strong, the habit fires only in specific contexts. Interventions can be precisely targeted to those contexts -- but also check PO-1-WorkSys and PO-3-Workflow for whether those contexts are modifiable.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If RM.1.5.3 (plan specificity) is low, habit formation depends on if-then cue linkage via BCT.1.4 (action planning). Without specific plans, BCT.8.3 (habit formation) has nothing to anchor to. Build RM-1.5-Intentions first.
- If PO.3.7 (activation energy) is high, even with cues and repetition the behavior is too hard to start. Reduce PO-3-Workflow friction via EN/ER before investing in habit-building.
- If AM.2.3.1 (valence) is strongly aversive, the behavior triggers dread or discomfort. Repetition alone won't override sustained aversive affect. Address AM-2.3-Affect (e.g. BCT.11.2, reduce negative emotions) before relying on habit formation.
- If SO.4.1 (reward alignment) favors the competing behavior, formal incentives will continuously undermine habit disruption. Realign SO-4-Incentives or the new habit will be displaced by the rewarded old one.

### AM-2.2-Reward: Reinforcement, Reward, and Wanting

**Core view of motivation:** Behavior is shaped by reward history, cue-triggered wanting, punishment, relief, and the reinforcement contingencies attached to action.

**Use when:** Revealing why some behaviors are strongly pulled forward in the moment, even when people know better -- especially persistence through reinforcement, craving, relief seeking, and reward salience.

**Diagnostic question:** What is being rewarded or made attractive in the moment?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **AM.2.2.1** Reward immediacy: delayed reward -> immediate reward
- **AM.2.2.2** Reward magnitude: weak reward -> strong reward
- **AM.2.2.3** Reinforcement frequency: rare reinforcement -> frequent reinforcement
- **AM.2.2.4** Negative reinforcement: little relief value -> strong relief value
- **AM.2.2.5** Cue-triggered wanting: low wanting -> high cue-driven wanting
- **AM.2.2.6** Extinction resistance: easy to extinguish -> highly persistent
- **AM.2.2.7** Goal-directed control: outcome-sensitive -> outcome-insensitive or habit-like

- When AM.2.2.5 (cue-triggered wanting) is high, environmental cues drive strong wanting. ER via BCT.12.3 (avoidance/reducing exposure to cues) to reduce trigger exposure. BCT.7.4 (remove access to the reward) to break the cue-reward link.
- When AM.2.2.4 (negative reinforcement) is high, the behavior provides relief from aversive states. BCT.11.2 (reduce negative emotions) to address the aversive state directly; BCT.8.2 (behavior substitution) to provide alternative relief.
- When AM.2.2.6 (extinction resistance) is high, the behavior persists even when reward is removed. Expect slow extinction. BCT.14.3 (remove reward) combined with BCT.14.8 (reward alternative behavior) to redirect rather than merely suppress.

- If SO.4.1 (reward alignment) formally rewards the unwanted behavior, individual-level INC for the target behavior is fighting the institutional structure. Realign SO-4-Incentives.
- If AM.2.1.1 (automaticity) is high, the behavior bypasses reward evaluation entirely. Address AM-2.1-Habit cue disruption alongside reward restructuring.
- If RM.1.1.1 (regulation quality) is controlled, external reward risks further undermining autonomous motivation. Use INC carefully and plan for transition to intrinsic reinforcement -- see RM-1.1-SDT.

### AM-2.3-Affect: Affect and Emotion

**Core view of motivation:** Immediate feelings such as anxiety, dread, pride, relief, or discomfort can drive or inhibit action independently of reflective plans.

**Use when:** Revealing hot-state approach and avoidance -- especially anxiety, discomfort, embarrassment, relief, and other feelings that make a behavior immediately hard or attractive.

**Diagnostic question:** What feelings pull the person toward or away from the behavior?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **AM.2.3.1** Valence: aversive -> attractive
- **AM.2.3.2** Arousal: calm -> highly activated
- **AM.2.3.3** Anticipatory affect: little emotional anticipation -> strong dread, hope, or relief expectation
- **AM.2.3.4** Mood influence: low mood dependence -> strong mood dependence
- **AM.2.3.5** Social-emotional intensity: low shame/pride/fear load -> high shame/pride/fear load
- **AM.2.3.6** Interoceptive burden: low bodily discomfort -> high bodily discomfort
- **AM.2.3.7** Emotion regulation capacity: reactions are manageable -> reactions easily dominate action
- **AM.2.3.8** Displacement guilt: low guilt about skipping -> chronic guilt from repeated intention-action gaps

**Shaping notes:**
- When AM.2.3.1 (valence) is aversive, the behavior triggers dread, disgust, or discomfort. BCT.11.2 (reduce negative emotions) to manage the aversive response. BCT.7.7 (exposure) for graduated desensitization if avoidance is the pattern. BCT.13.2 (framing/reframing) to reinterpret the emotional meaning.
- When AM.2.3.3 (anticipatory affect) involves strong dread, the person avoids before starting. BCT.15.2 (mental rehearsal of successful performance) to replace dread imagery with coping imagery. BCT.1.4 (action planning) with explicit coping plans for the emotional moment.
- When AM.2.3.5 (social-emotional intensity) is high with shame, pride, or fear dominating, BCT.3.3 (social support, emotional) to buffer the emotional load. BCT.13.4 (valued self-identity) to stabilize self-worth independent of performance.
- When AM.2.3.8 (displacement guilt) is chronic from repeated intention-action gaps, BCT.4.3 (re-attribution) to break the guilt cycle. BCT.1.4 (action planning) sized to achievable scope to restore intention-action alignment.

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.6.1 (voice safety) is low, affect may be driven by real political threat rather than irrational emotion. Address SO-6-Power -- the affect is a signal, not the problem.
- If PHC.2.3.2 (fatigue load) is high, emotional reactivity is amplified by exhaustion. Address PHC-2.3-Fatigue.
- If RM.1.2.1 (efficacy belief) is low, aversive affect around the behavior may be a downstream symptom of efficacy failure. Address RM-1.2-Efficacy -- affect may resolve once competence belief improves.

### AM-2.4-Helplessness: Learned Helplessness and Perceived Uncontrollability

**Core view of motivation:** When effort feels repeatedly disconnected from outcomes, people can become passive, disengaged, or resigned.

**Use when:** Revealing "why bother?" dynamics where people stop initiating effort despite having at least some capability and opportunity -- especially resignation, disengagement, futility, and motivational shutdown.

**Diagnostic question:** Has the person learned that trying does not matter?

**Dimensions it tends to foreground:**

- **AM.2.4.1** Perceived controllability: effort feels consequential -> effort feels futile
- **AM.2.4.2** Passivity: active coping -> withdrawal or resignation
- **AM.2.4.3** Generalization: helplessness is local -> helplessness spreads across situations
- **AM.2.4.4** Recovery expectation: setbacks feel temporary -> setbacks feel definitive
- **AM.2.4.5** Persistence: repeated attempts continue -> attempts collapse quickly
- **AM.2.4.6** Learned pessimism: outcomes seem influenceable -> outcomes seem fixed
- **AM.2.4.7** Exposure duration: short-term frustration -> prolonged uncontrollable experience

#### Pattern guidance

**Refs:** BCW: EN, PE | BCT: 2.2, 3.1, 3.3, 4.3, 4.4, 8.7, 15.1, 15.3 | Dims: RM.1.1.3, AM.2.4.1, AM.2.4.2, AM.2.4.3, AM.2.4.6, AM.2.4.7, PO.7.4, SO.4.1 | Cross: PO-7-Control, RM-1.1-SDT, RM-1.2-Efficacy, SO-4-Incentives

**Primary BCW linkage:** PE and EN. PE restores controllability via BCT.4.3 (re-attribution) to challenge fixed-outcome attributions and BCT.15.1 (verbal persuasion about capability). EN rebuilds agency via BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) for small undeniable successes and BCT.2.2 (feedback on behaviour) to make the effort-outcome link visible again.

**Shaping notes:**
- When AM.2.4.1 (perceived controllability) is low and AM.2.4.2 (passivity) is high, the person has stopped trying. BCT.4.3 (re-attribution) to challenge the fixed-outcome attribution. BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) to produce small, undeniable successes. BCT.2.2 (feedback on behaviour) to make the effort-outcome link visible again.
- When AM.2.4.3 (generalization) has spread, helplessness from one domain infects others. BCT.4.3 (re-attribution) to contain the attribution: "this was specific, not universal." BCT.15.3 (focus on past success) in adjacent domains.
- When AM.2.4.7 (exposure duration) is prolonged, deep-seated resignation requires slow rebuilding. BCT.8.7 (graded tasks) starting very small with guaranteed success. BCT.3.3 (social support, emotional) and BCT.3.1 (social support) for sustained encouragement.
- When AM.2.4.6 (learned pessimism) is entrenched, outcomes feel fixed. BCT.4.4 (behavioral experiments) to produce unexpected positive results that challenge the pessimistic model.
- Don't confuse AM-2.4-Helplessness with RM-1.2-Efficacy: efficacy is "I can't do this" while helplessness is "it doesn't matter what I do." The distinction shapes whether to build skill-confidence (PE via BCT.15.1) or restore controllability (PE via BCT.4.3).

**Redirecting notes:**
- If SO.4.1 (reward alignment) genuinely does not reward effort, helplessness may be an accurate reading of the environment. Fix SO-4-Incentives before asking the person to re-engage.
- If PO.7.4 (loop closure quality) is poor -- actions genuinely don't produce outcomes -- the system lacks controllability. Address PO-7-Control; the helplessness is rational.
- If RM.1.1.3 (autonomy support) is low and the environment is coercive, helplessness may be a response to loss of agency. Address RM-1.1-SDT and the controlling context.

## From Motivation Lenses To COM-B / BCW

These lenses do not replace COM-B. They help specify what kind of motivation problem is sitting inside the broad `M` branch.

- The reflective lenses mainly refine `RM`.
- The automatic lenses mainly refine `AM`.
- In practice, some situations blend both. For example, a person may have high `RM` but still be crowded out by low-value habits in `AM`, or they may have low `RM` and also lack any reinforcing habit loop in `AM`.

Once the dominant motivational pattern is clearer, the next step is usually to move into the BCW mapping in [com-b-to-bcw-intervention-function-mapping.md](../com-b-bcw-bct/com-b-to-bcw-intervention-function-mapping.md):

- `RM` is most often paired with `ED`, `PE`, `INC`, and `COE`.
- `AM` is most often paired with `PE`, `INC`, `COE`, `TR`, `ER`, `MO`, and `EN`.

The goal is not to duplicate that mapping here, but to make it easier to move from motivational diagnosis to intervention family.
